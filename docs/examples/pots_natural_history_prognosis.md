# Natural History and Prognosis of POTS (Including Post-COVID)

> **Research memo. Not medical advice.**
> This is the Bayesian prior against which every POTS intervention should be measured. If you don't know the expected trajectory without intervention, you can't attribute outcomes to treatment.

---

## Why This Matters

Intervention research for POTS often spans dozens of pages with zero information on the natural history or expected prognosis. Every intervention claim is implicitly benchmarked against "what would happen without it" --- and that benchmark is rarely defined. This memo fills that gap.

---

## Executive Summary

**The honest answer is: we don't know the long-term natural history of POTS in adults, and we especially don't know it for post-COVID POTS.** The most authoritative review (Raj, Fedorowski & Sheldon 2022, *CMAJ*) states this explicitly: *"Little evidence exists to inform the long-term natural history of POTS"* and *"No data exist on the natural history in adults."* [PMID: 35288409]

What we have instead is a patchwork of studies with conflicting results, severe selection biases, and no untreated control groups. The "optimistic" studies come from tertiary care centers (Mayo Clinic) where patients received specialized treatment. The "pessimistic" studies come from patient advocacy surveys where respondents self-selected for ongoing identification with the diagnosis.

**Best current estimate for classic POTS (all triggers):**
- ~37% no longer meet tilt-table criteria at 1 year (with treatment) [Kimpinski 2012]
- ~19% complete symptom resolution at 5 years (adolescent cohort) [Bhatia 2016]
- ~60% "functionally normal" at ~5.5 years (treated Mayo cohort) [Thieben 2007]
- ~99% report ongoing symptoms at mean 9.6 years (advocacy survey) [Boris 2024]

**Best current estimate for post-COVID POTS:**
- Prognosis formally unknown [Fedorowski 2023]
- 71.9% moderate-severe autonomic dysfunction at median 36 months [Miglis 2025]
- 81% of long COVID patients at 3 months still symptomatic at 12 months [RECOVER, Thaweethai 2025]
- 94% "improved" with multimodal treatment at median 5 months (case series, n=32) [Tsuchida 2023]

**Post-viral POTS may have a better prognosis than spontaneous-onset POTS.** Thieben 2007 found patients with antecedent events (including viral) did better than those with spontaneous onset. This is a key finding for any post-viral case and needs replication.

## The Evidence, Study by Study

### Tier 1: Prospective Studies with Objective Endpoints

#### Kimpinski et al. 2012 --- *Mayo Clin Proc* [PMID: 22795533; PMC3538485]
**The only truly prospective POTS follow-up study.**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Prospective cohort, 1-year follow-up |
| N | 58 enrolled, 54 completed |
| Demographics | 84% female, mean age 27.4 +/- 10.9 |
| Antecedent viral infection | 35% |
| Symptom duration at baseline | 41.0 +/- 52.8 months |

**Outcomes at 1 year:**
- 37% no longer met tilt-table criteria for POTS (HR increment <30 bpm)
- Mean HR increment: 37.8 -> 33.8 bpm (P = 0.12, NOT significant for whole cohort)
- 70% reported orthostatic symptom improvement
- 30% reported orthostatic symptom worsening
- COMPASS change score: -14.4 +/- 30.8 (improvement)
- Standing norepinephrine >600: 20% -> 6% (significant improvement in hyperadrenergic subset)
- Sudomotor denervation: 31% -> 36% (slight worsening)
- Adrenergic dysfunction: 21% -> 27% (slight worsening)
- Beta-blocker use increased: 35% -> 54%

**Evidence grade: B+** (prospective, standardized measures, but no control group, 1-year only, all patients treated at Mayo)

**Critical interpretation:** "No longer meeting criteria" does not equal "recovered." The mean HR increment didn't significantly change --- those who fell below the 30 bpm threshold may have been at the margin. Also, all patients received specialized care at a tertiary center + pharmacotherapy. This is an outcome *with treatment*, not a natural history.

---

#### Dipaola et al. 2020 --- *Int J Environ Res Public Health* [PMC7460485]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Prospective cohort, 2-year follow-up |
| N | 42 baseline -> 25 at 1yr -> 12 at 2yr |
| Demographics | 85.7% female, mean age 33.9 +/- 12.5 |

**Outcomes (n=12 with complete 2-year data):**
- COMPASS-31: 50.2 -> 45.0 (1yr) -> 43.6 (2yr) --- statistically significant
- Orthostatic intolerance domain: 25.7 -> 22.0 (1yr) -> 21.3 (2yr)
- Pupillomotor: significant improvement at 2yr
- GI, vasomotor, secretomotor, bladder: no significant change
- QoL: significant improvement on VAS at 2yr
- **0% of those who became occupationally inactive at baseline resumed work**

**Evidence grade: C+** (prospective, validated measures, but catastrophic attrition: 28.6% retention at 2 years. Direction of attrition bias is unknown --- dropouts may have improved and disengaged, or worsened and been unable to participate.)

**Critical interpretation:** Modest symptom improvement over 2 years that did NOT translate to functional recovery. The 0% occupational recovery rate is striking but based on only 12 completers. Don't overweight this number.

### Tier 2: Retrospective Cohorts with >100 Patients

#### Thieben/Sandroni et al. 2007 --- *Mayo Clin Proc* [PMID: 17352367]
**The most-cited "optimistic" POTS outcome study.**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Cross-sectional + retrospective follow-up questionnaire |
| N | 152 (records review) + 40 (questionnaire follow-up) |
| Demographics | 86.8% female, mean age 30.2 +/- 10.3 |
| Mean follow-up | 67 +/- 52 months (~5.6 years) |
| Antecedent viral illness | ~50% |

**Outcomes (n=40 follow-up questionnaire):**
- 80% improved
- 60% "functionally normal"
- 90% able to return to work
- Patients with antecedent events (viral) did BETTER than spontaneous onset
- Salt supplementation and beta-blockers were most efficacious therapies

**Evidence grade: C** (retrospective questionnaire, small n for outcomes, ~26% response rate from records, treated population, no standardized symptom scales)

**Critical interpretation:** This study is cited everywhere as "POTS has a good prognosis" but has serious problems:
1. N=40 for outcomes (of 152 records), response bias is severe
2. "Functionally normal" was self-reported on a non-validated questionnaire
3. All patients received specialized Mayo Clinic care
4. 67 +/- 52 months means huge variance (range ~15 months to ~10 years)
5. "Improved" does not equal "resolved" --- 80% improved but only 60% "functionally normal" means 20% improved but still not normal

**However:** The finding that post-viral POTS has better prognosis than spontaneous is biologically plausible (time-limited immune trigger vs. structural autonomic defect) and is the most decision-relevant finding for any post-viral case.

---

#### Boris et al. 2024 --- *JAHA* [PMID: 38958137; PMC11292765]
**The largest POTS outcomes survey.**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Cross-sectional online survey (Dysautonomia International) |
| N | 227 with sufficient data |
| Demographics | 85.5% female, mean age 21.8 +/- 3.5 at survey |
| Mean symptom duration | 9.6 +/- 3.4 years |
| Infection-triggered | 45.6% |

**Outcomes:**
- **99% reported ongoing symptoms** (only 2/215 reported zero symptoms in prior month)
- Median symptoms in past month: 13 (IQR 10-16.3)
- Female patients: 88.1% had >=10 symptoms vs 63.6% male (P = 0.0003)
- ~90% still require ongoing salt/water/exercise management
- SF-36 energy/fatigue score: 30 (IQR 15-45) --- severely impaired
- SF-36 general health: 35 (IQR 25-50) --- severely impaired

**Evidence grade: C** (cross-sectional survey, advocacy group recruitment = extreme selection bias toward persistent cases, no objective autonomic testing, self-report only)

**Critical interpretation:** This study directly contradicts Thieben 2007's "80% improved." The resolution is **selection bias operating in opposite directions**:
- Thieben: Mayo Clinic patients who responded to a follow-up questionnaire (selects for engaged, possibly improved patients)
- Boris: Dysautonomia International members still identifying as POTS patients ~10 years later (selects for people who still consider themselves ill)

Neither is a population-based sample. **The true long-term prognosis lies between 99% symptomatic (Boris) and 80% improved (Thieben), but we cannot place it more precisely.**

---

#### Bhatia et al. 2016 --- *J Pediatrics* [PMID: 26979650]
**Largest adolescent-onset POTS outcome study.**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Retrospective survey of Mayo Clinic patients |
| N | 172 respondents (34% of 502 surveyed) |
| Demographics | 84% female, mean age at survey 21.8 +/- 2.2 |
| Mean follow-up | 5.4 years (SD 1.9) |

**Outcomes:**
- 19% complete symptom resolution
- 51% persistent but improved symptoms
- 16% intermittent symptoms only
- 86% total: resolved, improved, or intermittent
- Physical composite QoL: 36.6 +/- 15.8 (population norm = 50, P < .001)
- Mental composite QoL: 50.1 +/- 11.2 (normal)
- 71% rated health as "good" or better

**Evidence grade: C+** (retrospective survey, 34% response rate, treated population, but larger N and standardized QoL measures)

**Critical interpretation:** Adolescent outcomes are probably better than adult. 86% improved is consistent with Thieben 2007 but from an adolescent-onset population where physiological maturation may contribute to improvement. **Cannot generalize to adult-onset POTS.**

### Tier 3: Post-COVID POTS Specific Data

#### Miglis et al. 2025 --- *Clin Auton Res* [DOI: 10.1007/s10286-025-01111-1]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Follow-up cohort, COMPASS-31 questionnaire |
| N | 526 adults with Long COVID |
| Median symptom duration | 36 months |

**Outcomes:**
- 71.9% scored >20 on COMPASS-31 (moderate-severe autonomic dysfunction)
- 40.5% newly diagnosed with POTS
- 37.5% could no longer work or had dropped out of school
- Autonomic symptom burden remained high at 3 years

**Evidence grade: C** (cohort follow-up, validated questionnaire, but no baseline comparison, Stanford clinic population = referral bias)

---

#### Tsuchida et al. 2023 --- *J Gen Fam Med* [DOI: 10.1002/jgf2.670]
**Most optimistic post-COVID POTS treatment data.**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Retrospective case series |
| N | 32 |
| Median follow-up | 159 days (~5.3 months) |

**Outcomes:**
- 94% showed improvement with multimodal treatment
- HR change on standing reduced 54% (38 -> 17.5 bpm)
- Employment/school: 31.3% -> 68.8%
- Treatment: exercise + compression + pharmacotherapy (midodrine, pyridostigmine, fludrocortisone)

**Evidence grade: C-** (case series, no control, short follow-up, single center, Japanese population)

**Critical interpretation:** This is the most encouraging data for post-COVID POTS treatment response. But 5 months is very short for a condition that waxes and wanes. Improvement may reflect natural recovery rather than treatment effect. Without controls, it's impossible to know.

---

#### RECOVER Adult Cohort (Thaweethai et al. 2025, *Nature Communications*)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Prospective multi-center cohort |
| N | 3,659 |

**Long COVID trajectory findings:**
- 10.3% had long COVID at 3 months post-infection
- 81% of those with long COVID at 3 months were still symptomatic at 12 months
- 8 distinct trajectories identified (not POTS-specific)
- ~5% had persistent symptoms throughout entire 15-month study

**Evidence grade: B** (prospective, large N, multi-center, but not POTS-specific, relatively short follow-up)

---

#### Al-Aly et al. 2024 --- *Nature Medicine* (3-year outcomes)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | VA medical records cohort |
| N | 135,161 COVID + 5.2M controls |
| Follow-up | 3 years |

**Key findings:**
- Non-hospitalized: excess mortality risk resolved after year 1
- Non-hospitalized: persistent pulmonary, neurologic, GI sequelae at year 3
- Hospitalized: elevated risks across all organ systems at year 3
- Not POTS-specific, but suggests autonomic/neurologic sequelae can persist >=3 years

**Evidence grade: B+** (large N, long follow-up, control group, but administrative data, not POTS-specific)

---

#### Rahmati et al. 2025 --- *J Med Virol* (3-year symptom meta-analysis)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Design | Meta-analysis, 11 studies |
| N | 142,171 |
| Follow-up | Up to 3 years |

**Symptom trajectory:**
- 20% had at least one unresolved symptom at 3 years
- Fatigue: ~28% at 6 months -> 11% at 3 years (declining)
- Dyspnea: 12% at 3 years
- Insomnia: 11% at 3 years

**Evidence grade: B** (meta-analysis, large combined N, but heterogeneous studies, not POTS-specific)

## Key Contradictions and Their Resolution

### Contradiction 1: "80% improved" vs "99% still symptomatic"

**Thieben 2007:** 80% improved, 60% functionally normal (N=40, Mayo)
**Boris 2024:** 99% ongoing symptoms, median 13 symptoms/month (N=227, advocacy survey)

**Resolution:** These are not measuring the same thing in the same population:
1. **Selection bias:** Thieben = treated patients who responded to follow-up. Boris = advocacy group members who still identify as POTS patients.
2. **"Improved" does not equal "asymptomatic":** A patient can be "improved" and "functionally normal" while still having some symptoms. Thieben's 60% functionally normal is not incompatible with those 60% reporting *some* residual symptoms.
3. **Temporal bias:** Boris's mean 9.6 years of symptoms selects for the tail of the distribution --- people who got better in 2-3 years don't join advocacy groups.

**True prior:** The proportion who become genuinely asymptomatic is probably **15-25%** (bracketed by Bhatia's 19% resolution and Thieben's 60% functionally normal minus the "improved but not resolved" group). The proportion who improve meaningfully is probably **50-70%**. The proportion with persistent significant symptoms is probably **30-50%**.

### Contradiction 2: Post-viral does better vs COVID POTS persists

**Thieben 2007:** Post-viral onset patients had better outcomes than spontaneous.
**Miglis 2025:** 71.9% moderate-severe at 36 months post-COVID.

**Resolution:** Not necessarily contradictory:
1. Thieben's post-viral follow-up was mean 5.6 years. Miglis is only 3 years. COVID POTS may still improve.
2. COVID may be a more severe autonomic insult than typical post-viral triggers.
3. The post-COVID population is larger and more heterogeneous, likely including cases that would have been diagnosed as "chronic fatigue" or "anxiety" pre-COVID.

**This is genuinely unresolved.** Whether post-COVID POTS follows the same trajectory as classical post-viral POTS is the single most important unanswered question.

## Predictors of Better Prognosis

Based on available evidence:

| Predictor | Source | Confidence |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| **Identifiable trigger (viral/post-infectious)** | Thieben 2007 | Medium --- single study, biologically plausible |
| **Shorter symptom duration before treatment** | Raj et al. 2022 | Medium --- stated in review, limited primary data |
| **Adolescent onset** | Bhatia 2016 | Medium --- physiological maturation contributes |
| **Male sex** | Bhatia 2016 (2x more likely to report recovery) | Low --- single study |
| **Mild baseline autonomic dysfunction (CASS <=3)** | Kimpinski 2012 | Low --- 95% had mild dysfunction at baseline, hard to differentiate |
| **Exercise tolerance / Levine protocol completion** | George 2016 | Medium --- completers 53-71% remission, but self-selection |
| **Lower norepinephrine (not hyperadrenergic)** | Kimpinski 2012 | Low --- hyperadrenergic subset improved NE but overall significance unclear |

## Practical Implications

1. **The "base rate" for meaningful improvement with treatment is roughly 50-70% at 1-5 years.** This is the prior against which to measure any specific intervention.

2. **The "base rate" for complete resolution is roughly 15-25%.** Most patients who improve still have residual symptoms.

3. **Post-viral onset is the single most favorable prognostic factor in the literature.** If Thieben 2007 is correct, post-viral POTS patients do better than spontaneous --- but this is one study from 2007 with N=40 and needs replication.

4. **Exercise is the only intervention with lasting autonomic remodeling evidence.** Levine protocol completers achieve 53-71% remission (George 2016), but only 29-48% on ITT. Exercise ramp-up is correctly prioritized in most treatment protocols.

5. **Most outcome studies measured patients ON treatment.** There is no evidence for what happens to untreated POTS patients. The "natural history" doesn't exist as a clean dataset --- everything we know is the trajectory of treated patients.

6. **Time horizon matters.** Most improvement appears to occur in the first 1-3 years. Boris 2024's 9.6-year cohort suggests that patients who haven't improved by then probably won't (survivorship in a chronic illness state).

---

## What the Literature Lacks

1. **No untreated POTS natural history study exists.** Every outcome study includes patients receiving some form of treatment. The spontaneous remission rate is unknown.
2. **No prospective study of post-COVID POTS beyond 3 years.** Miglis 2025 is the best at median 36 months.
3. **No head-to-head comparison of post-COVID POTS vs classical post-viral POTS trajectory.** The claim that "post-viral does better" is from pre-COVID studies and may or may not apply.
4. **No data on whether biomarker-guided treatment (e.g., ganglionic AChR antibody-positive -> immunotherapy) changes long-term trajectory.**
5. **No population-based incidence/recovery study.** All data comes from referral centers or advocacy groups.
6. **Dipaola 2020's 2-year outcomes are based on only 12 completers.** The 28.6% retention rate makes the data nearly uninterpretable.
7. **No data on POTS prognosis stratified by autoimmune markers.** Whether ganglionic AChR+ vs - POTS has different prognosis is unknown.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

| Study | N (outcomes) | Design | Grade | Key Bias |
|-------|-------------|--------|-------|----------|
| Kimpinski 2012 | 54 | Prospective 1yr | B+ | No control, treated population |
| Al-Aly 2024 | 135,161 | Retrospective 3yr | B+ | Not POTS-specific |
| RECOVER 2025 | 3,659 | Prospective 15mo | B | Not POTS-specific |
| Rahmati 2025 (MA) | 142,171 | Meta-analysis 3yr | B | Heterogeneous, not POTS-specific |
| Bhatia 2016 | 172 | Retrospective 5yr | C+ | 34% response, adolescent only |
| Dipaola 2020 | 12 | Prospective 2yr | C+ | 28.6% retention |
| Miglis 2025 | 526 | Cohort follow-up | C | No baseline, Stanford referral |
| Boris 2024 | 227 | Survey | C | Advocacy group = severe selection |
| Thieben 2007 | 40 | Retrospective | C | 26% follow-up response, non-validated |
| Tsuchida 2023 | 32 | Case series 5mo | C- | No control, short, single center |
| Fedorowski 2023 | Review | Review | --- | States "prognosis unknown" |
| Raj 2022 | Review | Review | --- | States "no data in adults" |

**Overall evidence quality for POTS prognosis: LOW.** No Grade A evidence exists. The best we have is one B+ prospective study (Kimpinski) for 1 year, with everything else being retrospective surveys or non-POTS-specific cohorts.
