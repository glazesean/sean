# Low-Dose Naltrexone for POTS and Post-COVID Fatigue — Research Summary

> Researched 2026-02-18. Agent-generated, verified against tool-retrieved sources.

## Bottom Line

**No controlled evidence for POTS autonomic improvement.** The Cleveland Clinic study (n=29) found no significant COMPASS-31 change. For fatigue/brain fog, the observational long COVID signal is consistent (Hedges' g ~0.5-0.9) but zero completed RCTs exist. The largest RCT in adjacent conditions (Bruun 2024, fibromyalgia, n=99) was negative.

## Direct POTS Evidence

### Zapata et al. 2025 (Cureus) — Cleveland Clinic
- **N = 29**, tertiary autonomic center, retrospective chart review
- 62% POTS, 34% general dysautonomia
- Dose: 86% started at 1mg, titrated to max 4.5mg
- **COMPASS-31: no significant improvement** (p=0.13, p=0.21)
- Pain: 24% improved, 31% no change, 14% worsened
- 5 patients (17%) reported mild side effects (insomnia, nausea, nightmares)

### Stallkamp Tidd et al. 2023 (Cureus)
- **N = 6**, retrospective case series, all female, tilt-table-confirmed POTS
- 3/6 reported subjective improvement, 2 discontinued for lack of benefit
- Too small for any conclusions

### Upcoming: NCT05363514 (University of Calgary)
- Phase 4 RCT, target 80 POTS patients
- 1.5mg → 3mg → 4.5mg over 16 weeks
- **Not yet recruiting** as of May 2024

## Long COVID Evidence

### Byambasuren et al. 2025 (medRxiv — Systematic Review)
- 4 pre-post observational studies, total n=155, **no RCTs**
- Pooled effect sizes (Hedges' g):
  - Fatigue: **g = -0.74** (95% CI: -1.11 to -0.37)
  - Pain: **g = -0.93** (95% CI: -1.29 to -0.57)
  - Brain fog: **g = -0.53** (95% CI: -1.01 to -0.05)
  - Sleep quality: **g = -0.60** (95% CI: -0.91 to -0.30)

### O'Kelly et al. 2022 (Brain Behav Immun Health) — Largest Published
- **N = 38** completed, single-center pre-post, Dublin
- Dose: 1mg month 1, 2mg month 2
- Significant improvements in recovery, pain, sleep, energy, concentration
- Only 2/38 (5.3%) discontinued
- **No control group** — cannot exclude placebo/natural recovery

### Tamariz et al. 2024 (Clinical Therapeutics)
- Retrospective cohort, n=108
- HR for improvement with LDN = 5.04 vs physical therapy alone
- Wide CI (1.22-20.77), selection bias likely

### Upcoming: NCT05430152 (UBC, Dr. Nacul) — THE KEY TRIAL
- Phase 2 RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled, target n=160
- Dose: 1mg titrating to 4.5mg over 16 weeks
- **Actively recruiting** (~1/3 completed). Results expected late 2026.

## ME/CFS Evidence (Adjacent)

### Younger et al. 2013 (Arthritis & Rheumatism) — Best RCT
- Crossover RCT, n=31 women with fibromyalgia
- Pain: LDN 28.8% vs placebo 18.0% (p=0.016)
- Mood and life satisfaction improved
- **Fatigue and sleep: NOT significantly improved**

### Tuominen et al. 2019 (Fatigue) — Largest Series
- Retrospective, n=218 ME/CFS patients, Finland
- **73.9% reported positive response** (improved vigilance, cognition, physical performance)
- Uncontrolled, self-reported

### Bruun et al. 2024 (Lancet Rheumatology) — KEY NEGATIVE
- RCT, **n=99** women with fibromyalgia, 6mg daily, 12 weeks
- **Not statistically significant** for pain
- Editorial: "Is LDN another treatment disappointment?"
- Caveats: 6mg may exceed "low dose" window; powered for 1.5-point difference

## Mechanism

Three proposed pathways:
1. **TLR4 antagonism / microglial modulation** — reduces central neuroinflammation. Best preclinical support.
2. **Endorphin rebound / OGF-OGFr axis** — brief opioid blockade triggers compensatory upregulation. Most commonly cited, less directly proven in humans.
3. **TRPM3 ion channel restoration** — restores NK cell calcium flux (Cabanas 2021). Newest, potentially most specific to ME/CFS.

## Practical Details

- **Timeline:** 2-8 weeks at target dose; total trial 8-12 weeks
- **Side effects:** Vivid dreams (10-30%), transient insomnia, mild nausea. No serious AEs at LDN doses.
- **Requires compounding pharmacy** (standard naltrexone is 50mg; LDN needs 1.5-4.5mg capsules)
- **Cost:** $30-60/month, not typically covered by insurance
- **Not a controlled substance** — any physician can prescribe
