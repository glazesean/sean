> Deep literature review across PubMed, bioRxiv, arXiv, and web sources. Last updated: 2026-02-19.

## 1. NR vs NMN -- The Definitive State of Evidence (Feb 2026)

### KEY FINDING: NR and NMN are roughly equivalent at raising blood NAD+; gut microbiome mediation is the new frontier

### 1a. Christen et al. -- First Head-to-Head-to-Head in Humans

**Christen et al. (Jan 2026) Nature Metabolism 8:62-73**
- DOI: 10.1038/s42255-025-01421-8 | PMID: 41540253
- **Design:** RCT, 65 healthy adults (avg age 34.7), 14 days
- **Arms:** NR 1g/day vs NMN 1g/day vs NAM 0.5g/day vs placebo
- **Result:** NR and NMN approximately **doubled** circulating NAD+ after 14 days. NAM only raised NAD+ acutely (4h post-dose), not sustained.
- **Microbiome finding:** Gut bacteria convert both NR and NMN to nicotinic acid (NA), which is the actual systemic NAD+ booster. Both NR and NMN (but not NAM) increased short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs).
- **Funder:** Nestle Health Science (all authors are Nestle employees)
- **Assessment:** CHANGES UNDERSTANDING. NR and NMN are functionally equivalent at equimolar doses for blood NAD+. The gut microbiome mediation is genuinely novel. **Confidence: HIGH** (well-designed, Nature Metabolism, but industry-funded)

### 1b. Berven et al. -- NR Outperforms NMN for Blood and Brain NAD+

**Berven et al. (2026) iScience**
- From University of Bergen, Norway (Tzoulis group)
- **Design:** Two-stage. Stage 1: 6 healthy adults, NR vs NMN 1200mg/day for 8 days. Stage 2: 6 healthy + 6 PD patients, NR 1200mg/day for 4 weeks.
- **Result:** NR raised blood NAD+ **2.3-fold more than NMN** (161% vs 69% increase after 8 days)
- **Brain NAD+:** Neither raised brain NAD+ in 8 days. After 4 weeks, NR significantly increased brain NAD+ in healthy participants.
- **Funder:** Not disclosed (academic group, previously linked to ChromaDex/NR research)
- **Assessment:** Small n=6, but the brain NAD+ finding is novel. Previous Tzoulis work has been NR-favorable. **Confidence: MEDIUM** (very small sample, potential investigator bias toward NR)

### 1c. NAD+ Precursor Review -- Nature Metabolism

**"NAD+ precursor supplementation in human ageing: clinical evidence and challenges" (Oct 2025) Nature Metabolism**
- DOI: 10.1038/s42255-025-01387-7
- Comprehensive review of all human NAD+ precursor trials
- **Conclusion:** NAD+ precursors reliably raise blood NAD+ but clinical benefits in healthy humans remain **modest and variable**. Exercise and metabolic health raise NAD+ more reliably than supplements.
- **Confidence: HIGH** (authoritative review in top journal)

### 1d. "Emerging strategies, applications and challenges of targeting NAD+ in the clinic" (Sep 2025) Nature Aging

- DOI: 10.1038/s43587-025-00947-6
- Review covering CD38 inhibitors, NAMPT activators, and combinatorial strategies beyond simple precursor supplementation
- Notes that tissue-specific NAD+ delivery remains unsolved

### 1e. MIB-626 (Pharmaceutical-Grade NMN) -- COVID/AKI Trial

**Pencina et al. (Jun 2025) FASEB BioAdvances 7(8):e70011**
- PMID: 40746868
- **Design:** Phase 2a RCT, 42 hospitalized COVID-19 patients with acute kidney injury, MIB-626 1g BID vs placebo for 14 days
- **Result:** MIB-626 safely and substantially raised blood NAD+ (16 -> 42.6 ug/mL by day 14). But **no significant difference** in kidney injury markers, inflammation (CRP, IL-6, TNFa), or disease severity.
- **Funder:** Metro International Biotech (David Sinclair-linked)
- **Assessment:** Confirms NMN raises NAD+ in sick patients, but zero clinical benefit. **Confidence: HIGH** (well-powered, negative result)

### 1f. NMN Cancer Concern -- Current Status

**Lerche et al. (May 2025) Photochemical & Photobiological Sciences 24:1069-1078**
- NMN increased tissue NAD+ in mice but **did NOT protect against UVR-induced skin cancer** (nor did it promote it)
- NNMT/nicotinamide pathway concern: NNMT overexpression in tumors is well-documented (Sun et al. 2024, Int Immunopharmacol; Wang et al. 2022, Cell Death Dis), but **no evidence that exogenous NMN supplementation drives this pathway in healthy tissue**
- aboutnad.com review (Feb 2026): "no studies demonstrating NMN causes cancer; NAMPT/NAD+ alterations in cancer cells are a consequence, not a cause"
- **Assessment:** The cancer concern is **theoretically plausible but empirically unsupported**. NMN neither promoted nor prevented cancer in the one animal model tested. **Confidence: MEDIUM** (absence of evidence is not evidence of absence; long-term human data lacking)

### 1g. Novel NAD+ Precursors (NRH, NMNH)

**NRH (dihydronicotinamide riboside):**
- Zeng et al. (Mar 2025) Metabolism 164:156110 -- NRH improves glucose/lipid metabolism in obese mice via adenosine kinase pathway
- Lee et al. (Feb 2025) Int J Mol Sci 26(4):1719 -- NRH enhances chemotherapy efficacy in ovarian cancer in vitro
- Vinten et al. (Feb 2026) FASEB J 40(4) -- Reduced vs oxidized NAD precursors drive **distinct** transcriptomic/proteomic profiles in hepatocytes
- **Status:** No human trials yet. NRH appears 2-4x more potent than NR at raising NAD+ in cell/animal models. Safety profile unknown in humans.
- **Assessment:** Promising preclinically but **years from clinical relevance**. **Confidence: LOW** for human use.

### 1h. Bryan Johnson's Protocol

- Johnson tested both NR and NMN for 90 days each (N=1, personal experiment, ~May 2025)
- Found both raised his NAD+ to "16-year-old levels" similarly
- Current 2025 protocol includes NR (1g/day) among ~100+ daily supplements
- **Assessment:** N=1 celebrity biohacking, not evidence. But directionally consistent with Christen et al.

### 1i. Peter Attia's Current Stance

- Huberman Lab podcast (Oct 2025): Attia classifies NAD+ precursors as "interesting but unproven"
- With Eric Verdin (Aug 2025): discussed possibility of taking NMN + NR together; Verdin says "don't give up on NAD+" but acknowledged limited clinical benefit data
- Attia sees a possible signal for skin (which has largest NAD+ decline with age)
- Still does NOT take NAD+ precursors himself (as of late 2025)
- **Assessment:** Expert skepticism maintained. Attia wants functional clinical endpoints, not just NAD+ blood levels.

### BOTTOM LINE FOR NR vs NMN:
- At equimolar oral doses, NR and NMN raise blood NAD+ similarly (Christen 2026)
- NR may have an edge for brain NAD+ (Berven 2026, very small study)
- Both work partly via gut microbiome conversion to nicotinic acid
- **Neither has demonstrated clinical benefits in healthy humans** in any well-powered RCT
- NR has regulatory advantage (GRAS, NDI) and more published safety data
- NMN has no long-term human safety data and lingering (unproven) cancer pathway concern

## 2. Urolithin A (Mitopure) -- Latest Trial Results

### KEY FINDING: New immune aging RCT published; expanding trial portfolio but all Amazentis-funded

### 2a. Denk et al. -- Immune Aging Trial (Nature Aging, Oct 2025)

**Denk et al. (2025) Nature Aging**
- DOI: 10.1038/s43587-025-00996-x | PMID: 41174221
- **Design:** RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled; 50 healthy middle-aged adults (49 completed); 1000mg UA daily for 4 weeks
- **Key findings:**
  - Expanded peripheral naive-like, less terminally exhausted CD8+ T cells (+0.50 percentage points, p=0.04)
  - Increased CD8+ fatty acid oxidation capacity (+14.72 percentage points, p=0.006)
  - Augmented mitochondrial biogenesis in CD8+ cells
  - Increased NK cells (CD56dimCD16bright)
  - Improved monocyte bacterial uptake
- **Funder:** Amazentis SA (manufacturer of Mitopure)
- **Assessment:** Novel immune endpoint, published in top journal. But small (n=50), short (4 weeks), and **entirely manufacturer-funded**. Immune markers are surrogate endpoints, not clinical outcomes. **Confidence: MEDIUM**

### 2b. Athletic Performance Trial

**Whitfield et al. (Aug 2025) Sports Medicine**
- PMID: 40839339
- **Design:** Double-blind, parallel-group, placebo-controlled; highly trained male distance runners; UA supplementation
- **Result:** Details pending full publication. Assessed running performance, recovery, and mitochondrial biomarkers.
- **Funder:** Likely Amazentis (Singh is co-author)
- **Assessment:** Extending UA claims to athletes. **Confidence: LOW** (incomplete data)

### 2c. Soccer Player Pilot Trial

**Monsalve Acevedo et al. (2025) Frontiers in Nutrition**
- PMID: 41245402
- **Design:** Pilot RCT, academy soccer players, 6-week preseason UA supplementation
- **Result:** Assessed performance outcomes, antioxidant status, and feasibility. Pilot-scale.
- **Assessment:** Small pilot. **Confidence: LOW**

### 2d. MitoEM Frailty Trial (Ongoing)

- Sponsor: Amazentis SA
- PI: Gilles Gouspillou
- Enrolling adults 65+, 8-week Mitopure supplementation
- Targeting muscle mitochondrial function in frail older adults
- Status: No longer recruiting (as of early 2026)

### 2e. ISRCTN47159002 (6-month Trial)

- Registered Oct 2025, 6-month duration
- No interim results published as of Feb 2026
- **Assessment:** Still in progress. Would be the longest UA supplementation trial if completed.

### 2f. Independent Trials?

- **No fully independent (non-Amazentis-funded) RCTs published as of Feb 2026**
- The Denk et al. paper (Nature Aging) had Frankfurt-based academic PIs but was funded and supplied by Amazentis
- This remains the biggest concern: the entire evidence base is manufacturer-controlled

### 2g. Negative/Null Results

- No published negative results for UA as of Feb 2026
- The athletic performance trial (Whitfield) is the most likely to show null results if UA doesn't extend to young/fit populations
- Publication bias concern is real

### BOTTOM LINE FOR UROLITHIN A:
- Growing clinical trial portfolio with 8+ published human studies
- Consistent biomarker improvements (mitochondrial markers, muscle strength, now immune markers)
- ALL funded by Amazentis/Timeline
- No negative results published (publication bias concern)
- No independent replication
- High-dose (1000mg) synthetic UA is needed because most people lack the gut bacteria to produce sufficient UA from dietary ellagitannins
- Mechanism (mitophagy induction) is well-supported in cell/animal models

## 3. Spermidine -- Human Evidence Update

### KEY FINDING: Oxford preprint shows immune benefits in humans; POLYCAD and Atria trials underway

### 3a. Alsaleh et al. -- Oxford Immune Aging Study (Preprint, Apr 2025)

**Alsaleh et al. (2025) Research Square (preprint, not yet peer-reviewed)**
- DOI: 10.21203/rs.3.rs-5686388/v1
- **Design:** Human trial in healthy older adults
- **Key findings:**
  - Spermidine mitigated immune cell senescence
  - Enhanced autophagy in immune cells
  - **Boosted vaccine responses** in healthy older adults
- **Funder:** Oxford University (academic, includes Wellcome Trust/Versus Arthritis funding)
- **Assessment:** THIS IS SIGNIFICANT -- first **independent** (non-industry) human trial showing immune benefits. Still a preprint. If confirmed, would be the strongest non-epidemiological human evidence for spermidine. **Confidence: MEDIUM** (preprint, awaiting peer review)

### 3b. POLYCAD Trial (Protocol Published Oct 2025)

**Thorup et al. (2025) Trials 26:452**
- PMID: 41168834 | DOI: 10.1186/s13063-025-09176-z
- **Design:** Danish RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled, HIGH-DOSE spermidine in elderly patients with coronary artery disease
- **Endpoints:** Cardiac remodeling, exercise capacity, muscle mass, systemic inflammation
- **Status:** Protocol published, recruitment ongoing (NCT06186102)
- **Assessment:** Most rigorous spermidine RCT ever designed. Will be the definitive clinical trial if completed. **Confidence: N/A** (not yet reporting results)

### 3c. Atria/ARGHI Healthspan Trial (Enrolling 2026)

- Peter Attia's Atria Research and Global Health Institute (ARGHI) is running a multi-arm trial
- **Interventions:** Spermidine + exercise (HIIT + resistance) + low-dose anti-inflammatory medications
- **Population:** Healthy adults aged 65-80
- **Rationale:** Targeting inflammaging through multiple pathways
- **Status:** Currently enrolling (Jan 2026)
- **Assessment:** High-profile trial backed by credible researchers. Multi-arm design makes it hard to isolate spermidine's independent effect. **Confidence: N/A** (enrolling)

### 3d. Cognitive Aging Review

**Yu et al. (2025) General Psychiatry 38(5):e101723**
- Systematic review of observational and interventional studies on spermidine and cognitive aging
- Concludes: consistent signal from epidemiological data but **insufficient RCT evidence**

### 3e. Spermidine + Protein Restriction (Animal)

**Liang et al. (Jun 2025) Aging (Albany NY) 17(6):1429-1451**
- PMID: 40489973
- Spermidine supplementation and protein restriction protect from organismal and brain aging **independently** in Drosophila and mice
- Important for mechanism: suggests spermidine works through autophagy independently of dietary restriction pathways

### 3f. Bruneck Cohort -- 20-Year Follow-up (Reference)

**Kiechl et al. (2018) Am J Clin Nutr 108(2):371-380**
- PMID: 29955838
- The 20-year Bruneck cohort data remains the strongest epidemiological evidence
- 829 participants aged 45-84, 341 deaths over 20 years
- Top vs bottom tertile of dietary spermidine: **39% lower mortality** (HR 0.76 per 1-SD increase)
- Equivalent to being 5.7 years younger
- No NEW follow-up published since 2018; no additional Bruneck publications on spermidine in 2025-2026

### 3g. Dose-Response and Product Quality

- SmartAge trial used wheat germ extract (Primeadine equivalent) at ~1.2mg spermidine/day
- POLYCAD trial uses "high-dose" (specific dose not yet disclosed)
- spermidineLIFE/Primeadine uses wheat germ extract standardized to 1mg spermidine per capsule
- No head-to-head comparison of wheat germ extract vs pure spermidine in humans
- **Assessment:** Dose-response data still lacking. The 1mg/day from supplements is far below dietary intake in the top Bruneck tertile (~80 umol/day from food).

### BOTTOM LINE FOR SPERMIDINE:
- Epidemiological evidence remains strong (Bruneck 20-year follow-up)
- Oxford preprint on immune benefits is the most exciting new development
- POLYCAD and Atria trials will be definitive if completed (2026-2027 timeframe)
- No completed RCTs beyond SmartAge showing clinical endpoints
- Dose-response in humans remains unknown
- Supplement doses (~1mg/day) may be inadequate vs dietary intake

## 4. Rapamycin / mTOR Inhibition for Healthy Humans

### KEY FINDING: PEARL trial published -- missed primary endpoint but sex-specific signals in women

### 4a. PEARL Trial Results

**Moel et al. (Apr 2025) Aging 17(4):908-936**
- PMID: 40188830 | DOI: 10.18632/aging.206235
- **Design:** 48-week, double-blind, RCT, 114 completers
  - Placebo: n=39
  - 5mg weekly compounded rapamycin: n=40
  - 10mg weekly compounded rapamycin: n=35
- **Population:** Healthy adults 50-85, only 35.1% women
- **Primary endpoint:** Visceral adiposity by DXA -- **FAILED** (p=0.942)
- **Key secondary findings:**
  - Women on 10mg: significant lean tissue mass gains at 24 and 48 weeks
  - Women on 10mg: significant improvement in self-reported pain
  - 5mg: improved general health perception
- **CRITICAL CAVEAT:** Compounded rapamycin had **~1/3 the bioavailability** of commercial formulations. Actual effective doses were much lower than intended.
- **Safety:** AEs comparable across groups; GI symptoms slightly more frequent with rapamycin
- **Funder:** AgelessRx; partially crowdfunded via Lifespan.io
- **Assessment:** The failed primary endpoint is definitive for visceral fat. The sex-specific signals in women are interesting but secondary/exploratory. The bioavailability issue undermines the entire dose-response interpretation. **Confidence: MEDIUM** (well-designed trial with a fundamental dosing problem)

### 4b. Compounded vs Commercial Rapamycin Bioavailability

**Harinath et al. (Jan 2025) GeroScience**
- PMID: 39873920
- 316 real-world rapamycin users + small trial cohort
- Compounded rapamycin: **~31% bioavailability of commercial** per mg
- Blood levels peak at ~2 days post-dose with gradual decline
- Substantial inter-individual variability
- **Assessment:** Critical pharmacokinetic data. Anyone using compounded rapamycin needs blood level monitoring. **Confidence: HIGH**

### 4c. Rapamycin + Trametinib Combination (Animal)

**Gkioni et al. (2025) Nature Aging**
- PMID: 40437307
- Rapamycin + trametinib (MEK inhibitor) combination was **additive** for lifespan extension in mice
- Reduced tumors, brain glucose uptake, and multi-organ inflammation
- **Assessment:** Interesting preclinical combination approach but years from human translation. **Confidence: HIGH** (for animal data)

### 4d. Rapamycin Review (Frontiers in Aging, Jun 2025)

**Roark & Iffland (2025) Front Aging 6:1628187**
- "Rapamycin for longevity: the pros, the cons, and future perspectives"
- Acknowledges gap between animal data and human evidence

### 4e. Systematic Evidence Review

**Hands et al. (2025) Aging 17**
- PMID: 40778880
- "What is the clinical evidence to support off-label rapamycin therapy in healthy adults?"
- **Conclusion:** Despite strong preclinical evidence, human data **have yet to establish** rapamycin as a proven geroprotector that can delay aging in healthy adults
- Calls for larger cohorts

### 4f. mTOR Pathway Variants and Longevity

**Kolbe et al. (Apr 2025) GeroScience 47:4955-4971**
- German longevity study: whole exome sequencing in 1245 long-lived individuals vs 4105 controls
- **Found significant enrichment of rare variants in mTOR-pathway genes** (RPS6, FLCN, SIK3) among centenarians
- Suggests that genetic mTOR dampening contributes to human longevity
- **Assessment:** Supports the mTOR inhibition hypothesis from a human genetics angle. **Confidence: HIGH**

**Sobczyk & Gaunt (2023, preprint) Mendelian Randomization of mTOR pathway:**
- Genetically proxied mTOR inhibition associated with OR=1.24 for top 1% longevity
- Reduced BMI, BMR, height; increased bone mineral density
- Little evidence for cardiovascular harm
- Notable mTOR expression QTL variants (e.g., rs1057079, rs2295080) exist, but **no specific pharmacogenomic studies linking them to rapamycin response have been published**

### 4g. Peter Attia on Rapamycin

- Classifies rapamycin as "promising" (strongest of the pharmacological candidates)
- With Brian Kennedy (Jul 2025): discussed ongoing human trials and dosing strategies
- Attia is known to have personally experimented with rapamycin but details of current use are private

### BOTTOM LINE FOR RAPAMYCIN:
- PEARL trial: missed primary endpoint; bioavailability problem makes results hard to interpret
- Women showed promising secondary signals (lean mass, pain)
- No well-powered human trial has shown rapamycin delays aging in healthy people
- Genetic evidence (German longevity study, MR studies) strongly supports mTOR dampening for longevity
- Compounded rapamycin needs blood level monitoring (~1/3 bioavailability of commercial)
- Young adults: essentially zero safety data for low-dose prophylactic use

## 5. Ergothioneine -- Accumulating Evidence

### KEY FINDING: Blue California RCT published with cognitive and sleep benefits; all evidence still manufacturer-linked

### 5a. Blue California RCT Published (Jun 2025)

**May-Zhang, Kuhn-Sherlock, Kakoschke, Zajac (2025) Nutraceuticals 5(3):15**
- DOI: 10.3390/nutraceuticals5030015
- **Design:** 16-week, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, **dose-ranging**
- **Population:** Healthy older adults with subjective memory complaints (n=147)
- **Doses:** Multiple doses tested (specific doses not fully detailed in available sources; likely 5mg, 25mg, and 50mg based on Blue California's research program)
- **Key findings:**
  - Improvements in cognitive function (memory tasks)
  - Improvements in sleep quality
  - Dose-response relationship observed
  - First dose-ranging human trial of ergothioneine
- **Funder:** Blue California (manufacturer of ErgoActive)
- **Assessment:** Largest ergothioneine RCT to date. Published in MDPI journal (lower tier). All authors linked to CSIRO (Australian government research org collaborating with Blue California). **Confidence: MEDIUM** (good sample size, dose-ranging design, but manufacturer-funded and in lower-tier journal)

### 5b. Cognitive Health Review (Cambridge, Nov 2025)

**May-Zhang (2025) Proceedings of the Nutrition Society**
- DOI: 10.1017/S0029665125101754
- "Ergothioneine for cognitive health, longevity and healthy ageing: where are we now?"
- Review authored by Blue California's Chief Science Officer
- **Assessment:** Industry review, not independent

### 5c. Telomere Length Preservation (In Vitro, May 2025)

- Blue California reported ErgoActive preserved telomere length and reduced telomere shortening under oxidative stress in vitro
- Published in Journal of Dietary Supplements
- **Assessment:** In vitro only, not human evidence

### 5d. Exercise + Ergothioneine Review (2026)

**Wazny et al. (2026) Ageing Research Reviews**
- PMID: 41390100
- Proposes combining ergothioneine + exercise for cognitive resilience
- Notes: no studies yet testing the combination
- **Assessment:** Hypothesis-generating, not evidence

### 5e. OCTN1 Transporter Genetics

**Key references:**
- Grundemann et al. (2005) PNAS 102(14):5256-5261 (discovery of OCTN1 as ergothioneine transporter)
- Urban et al. (2007) Pharmacogenetics Genomics 17(9):773-782 (functional effects of SLC22A4 variants)
- Frigeni et al. (2016) Biochim Biophys Acta 1860(6):1334-1342 (wide tolerance to amino acid substitutions)
- Lee et al. (Pharmacogenet Genomics, Singapore study) identified 24 SLC22A4 variants including 10 nonsynonymous; 4 predicted functionally significant (Arg63His, Arg83Pro, Met344Lys, Ile500Asn)
- **rs1050152 (L503F)**: most-studied variant, associated with Crohn's disease susceptibility; may have **reduced** ergothioneine transport
- **No pharmacogenomic study linking OCTN1 variants to ergothioneine supplementation response has been published**
- **Assessment:** The genetics of OCTN1-mediated ergothioneine absorption is understudied in the supplement context. Carriers of L503F (rs1050152) may absorb less. **Confidence: LOW** (extrapolation from transport studies, not supplement trials)

### BOTTOM LINE FOR ERGOTHIONEINE:
- Blue California RCT (n=147) shows cognitive/sleep benefits -- best human evidence to date
- All research is manufacturer-funded or manufacturer-connected
- OCTN1 genetics could affect absorption but no supplement-specific pharmacogenomics
- Mechanism (potent antioxidant, mitochondrial accumulation via OCTN1) is well-characterized
- No independent replication exists
- Dose-response being established (5-50mg range)

## 6. Taurine Longevity Update

### KEY FINDING: Major NIH study CHALLENGES taurine-as-aging-biomarker thesis; no human longevity RCTs

### 6a. Fernandez et al. -- "Is taurine an aging biomarker?" (Jun 2025, Science)

**Fernandez et al. (2025) Science 388(6751):eadl2116**
- DOI: 10.1126/science.adl2116
- **THIS CHANGES THE NARRATIVE**
- **Data:** Longitudinal human data (Baltimore Longitudinal Study of Aging, ages 26-100; Balearic Islands Study, ages 20-85; Atlanta cohort, ages 20-68), plus rhesus monkeys (ages 3-32) and mice (ages 9-27 months)
- **Key findings:**
  - Circulating taurine levels **did NOT consistently decline with age** -- often increased or remained constant
  - Within-individual variation **exceeded age-related changes**
  - Taurine levels were **inconsistently associated** with health outcomes across age, species, and cohorts
  - Association with muscle strength and body weight was unpredictable
- **Conclusion:** "Taurine supplementation to delay aging or broadly treat aging-related conditions may be context-dependent"
- **Funder:** NIH Intramural Research Program (fully independent)
- **Assessment:** THIS IS A DIRECT CHALLENGE to Singh et al. 2023 Science. The original paper showed taurine declines with age and supplementation extends lifespan in animals. This NIH follow-up says the decline is not universal and taurine is unlikely to be a reliable aging biomarker. **Confidence: HIGH** (NIH intramural, published in Science, longitudinal multi-species data)

### 6b. Alzheimer's/Taurine Transporter Connection

**Gavriel et al. (2025) Drug Discov Res**
- PMID: 40631607
- Found 2.8-fold lower SLC6A6 (taurine transporter) mRNA expression in AD lymphoblastoid cell lines vs controls (p=0.0005)
- Also reduced GPX1 (glutathione peroxidase) expression in AD
- Suggests taurine transport deficiency may contribute to AD
- **Assessment:** Interesting mechanistic finding but cell line data, not clinical. **Confidence: LOW**

### 6c. Ongoing Human Trials

**NCT06613542** -- "Effects of Daily Taurine Intake For 6 Months"
- Registered on ClinicalTrials.gov
- First dedicated taurine longevity/aging trial in humans
- No results yet

### 6d. Current State of Human Evidence

- Singh et al. (2023) Science: taurine extends lifespan in mice, worms, monkeys (observational in humans)
- Fernandez et al. (2025) Science: taurine levels don't reliably decline with age in humans
- **ZERO human RCTs showing taurine supplementation delays aging or extends lifespan**
- Animal doses that extended lifespan (500-1000 mg/kg/day in mice) would translate to 30-70g/day in humans -- far above supplement doses (1-3g)

### BOTTOM LINE FOR TAURINE:
- The Singh 2023 thesis is significantly weakened by Fernandez 2025
- Taurine is not a reliable aging biomarker
- No human RCTs demonstrate longevity benefit
- Animal-to-human dose translation is problematic (30-70g/day needed)
- Taurine supplementation at typical doses (1-3g) may have other benefits (cardiovascular, exercise) but the longevity-specific case is much weaker than it appeared in 2023

## 7. GlyNAC (Glycine + NAC) -- Latest

### KEY FINDING: No new major publications beyond Sekhar 2022-2023 RCTs; Sekhar group continues to dominate

### 7a. Published Evidence Base (Unchanged)

The GlyNAC evidence rests on **two main publications** from Rajagopal Sekhar's group at Baylor:

1. **Kumar et al. (2021) Clinical and Translational Medicine** -- Pilot trial in older adults. PMID: 33783984
2. **Kumar et al. (2023) J Gerontol A** -- Full RCT in older adults. Shows improvements in glutathione, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, inflammation, physical function, and aging hallmarks with 16 weeks of GlyNAC supplementation.
3. **Sekhar (2022) J Nutr** -- Additional analysis showing improvements in body composition, metabolic markers.
4. **PMC9767229 (2022)** -- GlyNAC protects from meal-driven oxidative stress and inflammation in older adults (sub-study of the RCT).

### 7b. Conference Presentations (2024-2025)

**Innovation in Aging (2024) GSA Conference Abstract:**
- "A PILOT CLINICAL TRIAL OF GLYNAC IN AGING" -- additional sub-analyses presented
- No full new publication

### 7c. GlyNAC vs NAC Alone

- No head-to-head comparison of GlyNAC vs NAC alone in a human RCT has been published
- Sekhar's theoretical framework: glycine deficiency is rate-limiting for glutathione synthesis in aging; NAC alone provides cysteine but glycine is needed too
- One mouse study (Sekhar group) showed GlyNAC superior to NAC alone for brain glutathione
- **Assessment:** The glycine addition is mechanistically plausible (glutathione = glycine + cysteine + glutamate) but **not proven superior to NAC alone in humans**. **Confidence: LOW** for superiority claim

### 7d. COVID-19 GlyNAC Trial

- NIH-funded R01 (AG054131-04S1) at Baylor for GlyNAC in older adults with COVID-19
- Targeted glutathione, inflammation, and recovery
- Results not yet published as of Feb 2026

### 7e. Independent Replication

- **No independent (non-Sekhar) groups have replicated GlyNAC findings in humans**
- This is the single biggest concern: all evidence comes from one lab

### BOTTOM LINE FOR GLYNAC:
- Compelling pilot and RCT data from a single group (Sekhar at Baylor)
- No independent replication
- No new major publications in 2025-2026
- Mechanism is sound (addressing rate-limiting glycine for glutathione synthesis)
- GlyNAC vs NAC alone: not tested in humans
- Cheap and accessible (glycine ~5g + NAC ~600mg, twice daily)
- For those already taking NAC, adding glycine is low-risk

## 8. Fisetin -- Latest Evidence

### KEY FINDING: First completed knee OA RCT presented; fisetin improved cartilage in aged mice; bioavailability still a major issue

### 8a. Fisetin Knee Osteoarthritis RCT (ORS 2025)

**Tashman et al. (Apr 2025) Osteoarthritis and Cartilage 33:S456**
- DOI: 10.1016/j.joca.2025.02.667
- **Design:** Phase I/II RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled (NCT04210986, Steadman Philippon Research Institute)
- **Population:** Mild to moderate knee OA
- **Intervention:** Fisetin (oral) -- likely intermittent dosing (senolytic protocol)
- **Status:** Completed; results presented at ORS 2025 as conference abstract
- **Full publication pending** -- only abstract available
- **Assessment:** First human senolytic RCT results for fisetin in OA. Full data needed. **Confidence: LOW** (abstract only, no full publication)

### 8b. Fisetin Reverses Endothelial Dysfunction (Preprint, Aug 2025)

**Mahoney et al. (2025) bioRxiv**
- Senolytic treatment with fisetin reverses age-related endothelial dysfunction partially mediated by SASP factor CXCL12
- Animal/cell study from CU Boulder group (Seals/Rossman/Clayton)
- **Assessment:** Strong mechanistic work from a credible group. Supports endothelial indication. **Confidence: MEDIUM** (preclinical)

### 8c. Murray et al. -- Fisetin Improves Physical Function in Aged Mice (Aging Cell, May 2025)

**Murray et al. (2025) Aging Cell e70114**
- Intermittent fisetin supplementation improved physical function and decreased cellular senescence in skeletal muscle with aging
- Compared to genetic senescent cell clearance and synthetic senolytics
- **Assessment:** Supports intermittent (senolytic) dosing over continuous. **Confidence: MEDIUM** (animal data)

### 8d. Pilot Trial in Healthy Volunteers (Ongoing)

**NCT06431932** -- "Pilot Trial of Fisetin in Healthy Volunteers and Older Adults"
- Status: Recruiting
- Will provide pharmacokinetic and safety data
- **Assessment:** Needed foundational data for dosing

### 8e. Bioavailability Issues

- Fisetin has notoriously poor oral bioavailability (~0.1% in standard form)
- Liposomal, micronized, and complexed formulations (e.g., with cyclodextrins) are being explored
- No published human PK data comparing formulations as of Feb 2026
- **Assessment:** Bioavailability remains the CRITICAL unsolved problem. Without improved delivery, oral fisetin may not reach therapeutic tissue levels. **Confidence: HIGH** (this is well-established)

### 8f. Senolytic vs Continuous Dosing

- Murray et al. (2025) supports **intermittent** dosing (senolytic hit-and-run approach)
- Most protocols use 2-day high-dose courses monthly or quarterly (e.g., 20mg/kg/day x 2 days)
- Continuous low-dose may have antioxidant/anti-inflammatory benefits but not senolytic
- **Assessment:** Intermittent high-dose is the consensus senolytic protocol. Continuous dosing shifts mechanism away from senolysis. **Confidence: MEDIUM**

### 8g. Fisetin Review -- Mayo Clinic Perspective

**Tavenier et al. (2024) -- "Fisetin as a senotherapeutic agent"**
- From Kirkland/Tchkonia group at Mayo Clinic
- Comprehensive review of in vitro, animal, and early clinical evidence
- Acknowledges bioavailability as key limitation
- Notes that fisetin preferentially clears senescent cells over healthy cells

### BOTTOM LINE FOR FISETIN:
- First completed RCT results (OA, conference abstract only)
- Bioavailability is the critical unsolved problem
- Intermittent high-dose protocol is the emerging consensus for senolytic use
- Strong preclinical data for endothelial dysfunction and physical function
- Still no published human RCT with full results showing clinical benefit
- Multiple trials ongoing (OA, healthy volunteers)
- Cheap (~$0.10/gram), widely available, but formulation matters enormously

## What CHANGES Current Understanding (vs pre-2025)

1. **NR and NMN are equivalent** for blood NAD+ at equimolar doses (Christen 2026). The NR-is-superior narrative from some researchers is overstated (Berven study was n=6).

2. **Taurine as aging biomarker is challenged** by NIH (Fernandez 2025, Science). The Singh 2023 thesis is significantly weakened. Taurine supplementation for longevity is much less well-supported than it appeared.

3. **Rapamycin PEARL trial failed** its primary endpoint. The bioavailability issue with compounded rapamycin means we still don't know whether properly-dosed rapamycin helps healthy humans.

4. **mTOR pathway variants** are enriched in centenarians (Kolbe 2025, German longevity study), strengthening the genetic case for mTOR inhibition even as the pharmacological case struggles.

5. **Urolithin A** now has immune aging data (Denk 2025) expanding beyond muscle, but independence concern is unchanged.

6. **Spermidine** Oxford preprint showing immune benefits in humans is the most exciting independent finding across all compounds reviewed.

7. **Fisetin** OA trial results are in (abstract only) -- first human senolytic RCT completed. Full publication awaited.

8. **GlyNAC** evidence is stagnant -- no new publications from the single lab producing all the evidence.
